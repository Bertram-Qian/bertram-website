The brief
A calorie tracker that doesn't feel like a chore
Most nutrition apps feel clinical — spreadsheets with a logo. Trim was designed from scratch to feel warm, fast, and genuinely encouraging. The challenge: make logging food feel lighter than not logging it at all.

From concept to execution
How Trim was designed
A structured 8-week process from user research through to production-ready handoff, with motion prototyping woven throughout — not bolted on at the end.
1
Discovery & research
Interviewed 12 people who had tried and abandoned calorie trackers. Ran competitive audits of MyFitnessPal, Cronometer, and Lose It. Identified three core failure modes: friction, shame, and forgettability.
Key insight
"I stopped because every time I opened it I felt like I'd already failed."
— Research participant, 28, product manager
2
Concept exploration
Explored three distinct design directions: Clinical (data-dense, precision-first), Playful (gamified, character-driven), and Editorial (warm, typographic, calm). User testing clearly favoured Editorial — it felt like something you'd want to open.

Visual language & typography
Chose Fraunces (variable optical-size serif) paired with Geist (geometric sans). The pairing creates an editorial warmth rare in utility apps. Defined a restricted palette of four semantic colour tokens — herb, terracotta, slate, sand — each with a clear role.

Motion-first prototyping

5
Component build & system
Extracted every repeating pattern into a token-driven component library. Documented motion values, spacing scale, colour roles, and icon spec. Built 22 components, 4 icon weights, and 6 animation presets.
6
Usability testing & iteration
Ran two rounds of moderated testing with 8 participants each. Key changes: increased tap targets on meal cards, added meal-type selector to log food modal, simplified macro display from 4 values to 3, added chevron affordance to expandable cards.
7
Handoff & documentation
Delivered annotated specs, a motion reference doc with easing curves and durations, a component library with all states (default, hover, active, disabled, loading), and a responsive breakpoint guide.
Motion design system
Purposeful, not decorative
Every animation in Trim follows three principles: it must communicate state, guide attention, or reward action. Motion that does none of these was cut.

Motion principles
Entrance communicates readiness
Elements that animate in signal they are interactive and loaded. Static screens feel broken. All primary content uses a staggered fade-up on load.
Duration respects attention
Micro-interactions (toggles, taps) complete in under 220ms — imperceptible as "animation" but perceptible as response. Longer draws (ring, chart) are intentionally slow because they carry emotional weight.
Spring = user-initiated, ease = system-initiated
When the user triggers an action (tap a card, add food), the response uses a spring curve — it feels alive. When the system does something (page loads, data arrives), standard ease is used — it feels controlled.
Stagger creates hierarchy
When multiple elements enter together (recipe grid, meal items), they stagger at 60–80ms intervals. This creates a reading order and prevents visual noise without requiring the designer to specify layout hierarchy separately.

Platform-aware design
Same product, different expectations
Trim targets iOS, Android, and responsive web. Each platform has different conventions for navigation, gestures, and motion. The design system was built to accommodate all three without fragmenting the visual language.
iOS
Follows HIG conventions where they align with Trim's aesthetic. Departs intentionally on navigation (no tab bar labels visible by default — shown on active), typography (system font replaced with Geist + Fraunces), and spring curves (iOS uses UISpring; Trim defines its own).

Accessibility
Colour contrast
All text meets WCAG AA (4.5:1 for body, 3:1 for large). The herb green (#5A7A58 on white) achieves 4.8:1. Colour is never the only differentiator — shape, position, and label always accompany colour signals.
Motion accessibility
All animation respects prefers-reduced-motion. When enabled, durations collapse to 0ms — layout is identical, only timing is removed. No information is conveyed through motion alone.
Design system
Tokens, not one-offs
Everything in Trim is derived from a token hierarchy. There are no magic numbers in the code — every value references a named token. This means a single change to --herb propagates correctly across every component.

Key learnings
Motion should be designed before visuals are finalised. Prototyping the ring entrance and card interactions early shaped layout decisions — the ring size, the card padding, the chevron placement were all informed by how the motion felt.
Tone is a design decision, not a copy decision. The choice of Fraunces as a display face — especially its italic — was the single biggest contributor to the app feeling warm rather than clinical. Typography was more impactful than colour.
Platform conventions exist for good reasons. The impulse to use a full-screen modal was overridden by iOS convention (bottom sheet). Users in testing consistently tried to swipe down to dismiss — the platform expectation was stronger than the design intent.
A token system pays off immediately, not eventually. When the terracotta shade needed adjusting after accessibility testing, one variable change fixed every over-goal bar, every warning state, and every terr-tagged recipe card simultaneously.
